About the project

This project is an international collaboration between Information Technologies students from the Águeda Higher School of Technology and Management (ESTGA) - University of Aveiro, in Portugal, and the Topographic Engineery students from University of Costa Rica. The goal is to transform LiDAR data into a point cloud that can be visualized on the web, in 3D. In addition, the project also involves counting the number of trees present in each Tile.

Study Area

The city of Mafra was chosen as the study area for this project, within a catalog of 7 territories (Mafra, Parque Florestal de Monsanto, Pombal, Proença-a-Nova, Serras de Lousã, Sintra-Cascais, Vila Pouca de Aguiar). Mafra offers a unique combination of urban and forest environments, making it ideal for data collection.

Phase 1: DTM and DSM

In the first phase of the project, the students from the University of Costa Rica were responsible for determining the Digital Terrain Model (DTM), the Digital Surface Model (DSM), and the tree count of the terrain, in this case, every 5 meters. To do this, they used QGIS software, along with the WhiteboxTools and LAStools extensions.

Phase 2: Web Visualization

In the second phase of the project, the students from the University of Aveiro (ESTGA) were responsible for implementing the web visualization of the data obtained in the first part. For this, they used the Cesium library and web programming languages ​​(HTML/CSS/JS).

Positive Aspects

There were several positive aspects that contributed to the success of this project. First and foremost, there was a good synergy and communication between the group members, which facilitated collaboration and problem-solving. The tasks were executed within the established deadlines, ensuring that the project stayed on track. Additionally, the team was able to research and gather knowledge about QGIS and LiDAR, expanding their skill set and contributing to the project's objectives.

Challenges

Despite the positive aspects, the team also faced some challenges during the project. One of the main difficulties was the time difference between Portugal and Costa Rica, which made scheduling meetings and coordinating work more complex.

Conclusion

This project provided a rich learning opportunity for all involved. We learned the importance of communication and synergy in a team environment, especially when working across different time zones. We learned to manage our time effectively to meet established deadlines. Additionally, we gained valuable knowledge about QGIS, LiDAR and Web Mapping, which are important tools in the field of Geographic Information Systems (GIS). We also familiarized ourselves with the Cesium library for web data visualization. Despite the challenges, we managed to overcome them and in doing so, gained a greater appreciation for problem-solving and international collaboration. This project not only enhanced our technical skills but also taught us valuable lessons about teamwork and collaboration that we will carry with us into our future endeavors.
